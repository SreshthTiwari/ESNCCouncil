Apollo

Apollo is one of the Olympian deities in classical Greek and Roman mythology. The national divinity of the Greeks, Apollo has been recognized as the Greek god of the sun, light, healing, disease, plague, music, art, poetry, archery, reason, knowledge, truth, and prophecy. He is the twin brother of the goddess Artemis.

Zeus

Zeus is the sky and thunder god in ancient Greek religion, who rules as king of the gods of Mount Olympus. His name is cognate with the first element of his Roman equivalent Jupiter. His mythology and powers are similar, though not identical, to those of Indo-European deities such as Jupiter, Perkūnas, Perun, Indra and Dyaus.

Poseidon

Poseidon was on of the Twelve Olympians in ancient Greek religion and myth, god of the sea, storms, earthquakes and horses. In pre-Olympian Bronze Age Greece, he was venerated as a chief deity at pylos and Thebes. He had also the cult title "earth shaker". His Roman equivalent is Neptune. Poseidon was protector of seafarers, and of many Hellenic cities.

Mercury

Mercury - son of Maia and Jupiter - was the Roman god of commerce, often serving as a mediator between the gods and mortals, his winged feet giving him the the advantage of speed.
Mercury is often associated with the Greek god Hermes - who is considered as a counterpart.
